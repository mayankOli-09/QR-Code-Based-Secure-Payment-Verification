      Frequently Asked Questions
      
          
      Everything you need to know about QR payment safety and this tool.
      
        
      

        
      

          
      
            
            
      
              
      QR codes are just encoded text — anyone can create one. Scammers frequently replace legitimate merchant QR codes with their own, so when you scan and pay, the money goes to the fraudster instead of the actual merchant. By verifying a QR code before paying, you confirm that the UPI ID, payee name, and amount are genuine and match who you intend to pay. A 5-second check can save you from losing money with no way to recover it.
      
            
      
          
      

          
      
            
            
      
              
      Payment screenshots are trivially easy to fake. Anyone can edit a screenshot using basic photo editing tools to change the amount, payee name, or status. Merchants who accept screenshots as proof of payment without verification risk releasing goods or services without ever receiving the actual money. Our screenshot checker uses OCR to extract text, verifies transaction IDs, detects known UPI app signatures, and flags low-quality or edited images — giving merchants a reliable second opinion before they act on a receipt.
      
            
      
          
      

          
      
            
            
      
              
      Quishing (QR code phishing) is a cyberattack where scammers embed malicious URLs inside QR codes. When you scan the code, instead of going to a legitimate payment page, you are redirected to a fake website designed to steal your UPI PIN, bank credentials, or personal information. Quishing is particularly dangerous because most QR scanner apps do not display the full URL before redirecting you. Our tool decodes the QR first and checks the embedded URL against offline heuristics, VirusTotal, and Google Safe Browsing before you visit anything.
      
            
      
          
      

          
      
            
            
      
              
      QR code swapping is one of the most common forms of payment fraud in India. A scammer visits a shop, restaurant, or stall and sticks their own QR code printout over the merchant's original one. Customers unknowingly scan the fake code and pay the scammer. The merchant receives no payment but believes they have. This type of fraud is nearly impossible to detect with the naked eye — which is exactly why verifying the payee name and UPI ID in the QR before paying is critical.
      
            
      
          
      

          
      
            
            
      
              
      UPI transactions are near-instantaneous and generally irreversible. Once money leaves your account to a fraudulent UPI ID, recovery is extremely difficult. You can file a complaint with your bank or on the NPCI Dispute Redressal Portal, but outcomes are not guaranteed and the process can take weeks. The only reliable protection is prevention — verifying a QR code before paying takes seconds and eliminates this risk entirely.
      
            
      
          
      

          
      
            
            
      
              
      The Trust Score is a number between 0 and 100 that represents how safe a QR code appears based on multiple security checks. A score of 80–100 is SAFE, 50–79 is WARNING, and 0–49 is DANGEROUS. It is calculated by evaluating factors such as whether the UPI ID format is valid, whether the payee name is present, whether the VPA suffix belongs to a known bank or wallet, whether the embedded URL is clean, and whether the transaction amount appears unusual. The score gives you a quick, at-a-glance indication of risk.

Your QR code images and payment screenshots are sent only to the backend server running on your own device (127.0.0.1) and are never uploaded to any external server or stored permanently. If URL checking is enabled with API keys, only the URL string (not your image) is sent to VirusTotal or Google Safe Browsing for analysis.
      
            
      
          
      

          
      
            
            
      
              
      The screenshot checker can detect receipts from Google Pay, PhonePe, Paytm, BHIM, Amazon Pay, Mobikwik, and Razorpay. It looks for app-specific branding, transaction ID formats, and layout patterns unique to each platform. If the app is not detected, the system still attempts to extract transaction details and gives a confidence score based on what it finds.
      
            
      
          
      

          
      
            
            
      
              
      A SUSPICIOUS verdict means the system could not fully verify the QR code or screenshot, but also could not confirm it is safe. This usually happens when some expected elements — like a transaction ID, payee name, or known app signature — are missing or unclear. In this case, you should ask the payer to share the UPI transaction reference number directly from their bank app, or verify the QR code payee details manually before proceeding.

Always cross-check the payee name shown in your payment app against who you intend to pay, and never enter your UPI PIN on any website. This tool is designed to significantly reduce your risk, not replace your own judgment entirely.

      About QR Pay Verifier
      
          
      A security tool built to fight payment fraud before it happens.
      
        
      

        
      
          
      Why this project exists
      
          
      India processes over 10 billion UPI transactions every month. With this scale comes an explosion of QR-based payment fraud — fake QR codes placed over legitimate ones, malicious URLs hidden inside payment codes, and fabricated payment screenshots used to deceive merchants. Existing UPI apps do not verify QR codes before you pay, and they offer no way for merchants to authenticate a payment screenshot. QR Pay Verifier was built to fill this gap.

      What happens if you don't verify
      
          
      
            
      
              
      💸
      
              
      
                
      Money sent to wrong UPI ID
      
                
      A swapped QR code at a petrol pump, tea stall, or shop silently redirects your payment. The merchant gets nothing. You lose the money. UPI transfers are instant and almost never reversible.
      
              
      
            
      
            
      
              
      🖼️
      
              
      
                
      Accepting a fake payment screenshot
      
                
      A buyer sends you an edited screenshot showing ₹5,000 paid. You release the product. No money ever arrives in your account. By the time you realize, the buyer is unreachable.
      
              
      
            
      
            
      
              
      🎣
      
              
      
                
      Quishing — phishing via QR code
      
                
      You scan a QR code on a poster, menu, or parking meter. Instead of a payment page, you land on a fake bank login site that steals your credentials and empties your account.
      
              
      
            
      
            
      
              
      ⏳
      
              
      
                
      No guaranteed way to recover funds
      
                
      Complaint resolution through NPCI or your bank can take weeks with no guaranteed outcome. Prevention is the only reliable protection.
      
              
      
            
      
          
      
        
      

        
      
          
      Real-world examples of this fraud
      
          
      
            
      
              
      QR Swap Fraud
      
              
      In 2023, police in Bengaluru busted a gang that had systematically replaced QR codes at over 200 small shops across the city, redirecting payments for months before being caught. Merchants collectively lost lakhs of rupees.
      
            
      
            
      
              
      Fake Screenshot Fraud
      
              
      A common scam on OLX and Facebook Marketplace involves buyers sending fake Paytm or GPay screenshots for second-hand goods. Sellers ship items believing payment was made. The screenshots are edited images with no actual transaction behind them.
      
            
      
            
      
              
      Quishing Attack
      
              
      Fraudulent QR codes were found pasted on parking meters in several Indian cities, redirecting users to fake payment portals that harvested UPI PINs and bank credentials instead of processing parking fees.
      
            
      
            
      
              
      UPI Overpayment Scam
      
              
      Scammers send a "request to pay" QR code instead of a payment QR, tricking victims into scanning it and unknowingly sending money rather than receiving it — a fraud that specifically targets people selling items online.
